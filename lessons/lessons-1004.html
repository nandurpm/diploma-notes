1. Atomic Structure

CO1M1.01

An atom is the smallest particle of an element that shows the chemical properties of that element. It contains a central nucleus and electrons around it.

Parts of an Atom

ParticleChargeLocation
ProtonPositive (+)Nucleus
NeutronNo chargeNucleus
ElectronNegative (-)Shells / orbitals

Bohr's Atom Model

  • Electrons revolve around the nucleus in fixed circular paths called shells.
  • Each shell has fixed energy.
  • Energy is absorbed or emitted when an electron jumps from one shell to another.
Simple Atom Diagram

Dual Nature of Matter

Matter can show both particle nature and wave nature. According to de Broglie, a moving particle has a wavelength.

λ = h / mv

Where λ = wavelength, h = Planck's constant, m = mass, v = velocity.

Heisenberg's Uncertainty Principle

It is impossible to accurately know both the exact position and exact momentum of a small particle like an electron at the same time.

Δx × Δp ≥ h / 4π

Quick Revision

  • Who proposed fixed energy levels? Bohr.
  • Which particle has negative charge? Electron.
  • What is the meaning of dual nature? Matter behaves as both particle and wave.

2. Orbitals & Electronic Configuration

CO1M1.02

An orbital is the region around the nucleus where there is a high probability of finding an electron.

Quantum Numbers

Quantum NumberMeaning
Principal (n)Main shell / energy level
Azimuthal (l)Subshell shape: s, p, d, f
Magnetic (m)Orientation of orbital
Spin (s)Spin direction of electron

Important Rules

  • Aufbau rule: Electrons fill lower energy orbitals first.
  • Pauli exclusion principle: One orbital can hold maximum two electrons with opposite spin.
  • Hund's rule: Electrons occupy equal energy orbitals singly before pairing.

Order of Filling Orbitals

1s → 2s → 2p → 3s → 3p → 4s → 3d → 4p

Hydrogen11s¹

Oxygen81s² 2s² 2p⁴
Sodium111s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s¹

3. Chemical Bonding

CO1M1.03

Chemical bonding is the force of attraction that holds atoms together in a molecule or compound.

Octet Rule

Atoms combine with other atoms to get 8 electrons in their outermost shell. This stable arrangement is called an octet.

Types of Bonds

BondSimple MeaningExample
Ionic bondElectron transfer from one atom to anotherNaCl
Covalent bondSharing of electron pairsH₂, HF
Coordinate bondShared pair is donated by one atom onlyNH₄⁺ ion
Hydrogen bondingWeak attraction involving hydrogenH₂O

Why Water is Special

Water shows anomalous behaviour because of hydrogen bonding. Hydrogen bonding gives water high boiling point, surface tension and unusual expansion on freezing.

Ionic Bond Example: NaCl
Na loses 1 electron→ Na⁺+ Cl gains 1 electron→ Cl⁻→ NaCl

Quick Revision

  • NaCl has ionic bond.
  • H₂ has covalent bond.
  • NH₄⁺ has coordinate bond.
  • Water has hydrogen bonding.

4. Solutions & Concentration

CO2M2.01

A solution is a homogeneous mixture of solute and solvent.

Basic Terms

  • Solute: Substance dissolved in the solution.
  • Solvent: Substance that dissolves the solute.
  • Solution: Mixture of solute and solvent.

Example

In salt water, salt is the solute and water is the solvent.

Methods to Express Concentration

MethodFormula / Meaning
Molarity (M)Moles of solute per litre of solution
Normality (N)Gram equivalent of solute per litre of solution
ppmParts per million; used for very dilute solutions
Molarity = Number of moles of solute / Volume of solution in litre
Normality = Gram equivalent of solute / Volume of solution in litre
ppm = (Mass of solute / Mass of solution) × 10⁶

Quick Practice

Question: How many moles are present in 1 litre of 1 M solution?

Answer: 1 mole.

5. pH, pOH & Buffer Solutions

CO2M2.02

pH is a number used to express whether a solution is acidic, basic or neutral.

pH Scale

pH ValueNatureExample
Less than 7AcidicLemon juice
7NeutralPure water
More than 7BasicSoap solution

Important Relation

pH + pOH = 14

This relation is valid for aqueous solutions at 25°C.

Buffer Solution

A buffer solution resists change in pH when a small amount of acid or base is added.

  • Acidic buffer: Weak acid + its salt. Example: CH₃COOH + CH₃COONa.
  • Basic buffer: Weak base + its salt. Example: NH₄OH + NH₄Cl.
Application: pH is important in water testing, agriculture, blood, medicine, food processing and industrial processes.

Quick Practice

If pH = 5, then pOH = 14 - 5 = 9.

6. Volumetric Analysis

CO2M2.01

Volumetric analysis is a quantitative method used to find the concentration of a solution by measuring volumes.

Important Terms

  • Titration: Process of adding one solution to another to complete a reaction.
  • End point: Stage where the reaction is just complete.
  • Indicator: Substance that changes colour at the end point.

Normality Equation

N₁V₁ = N₂V₂

N = normality, V = volume.

Simple Titration Setup
Burette solution↓ Conical flask solution + indicator→ Colour change at end point
Titration TypeCommon Indicator
Strong acid vs strong basePhenolphthalein or methyl orange
Strong acid vs weak baseMethyl orange
Weak acid vs strong basePhenolphthalein

7. Water Hardness & Treatment

CO2M2.03 & M2.04

Hard water does not easily form lather with soap. Hardness is mainly caused by calcium and magnesium salts.

Types of Hardness

TypeCauseRemoval
Temporary hardnessBicarbonates of Ca and MgBoiling / lime treatment
Permanent hardnessSulphates and chlorides of Ca and MgSoda lime / ion exchange

Problems in Boilers

  • Scale formation reduces heat transfer.
  • Corrosion damages boiler metal.
  • More fuel is needed due to poor heat transfer.

Water Softening Methods

  • Soda lime process: Adds lime and soda to remove hardness-causing ions as precipitates.
  • Ion exchange method: Replaces Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ ions with Na⁺ or H⁺ ions using resin.
Municipal Water Treatment Flow Chart
Raw water→ Sedimentation→ Coagulation→ Filtration→ Sterilization→ Potable water
Potable water: Water safe for drinking. It should be clear, colourless, odourless and free from harmful microorganisms.

8. Alloys, Glasses & Refractories

CO3M3.01

Engineering materials are selected based on strength, cost, corrosion resistance, heat resistance and application.

Alloys

An alloy is a mixture of two or more elements, where at least one is a metal.

  • Brass: Copper + Zinc. Used in taps, screws and decorative items.
  • Bronze: Copper + Tin. Used in bearings, bells and statues.
  • Solder: Tin + Lead. Used for joining electrical parts.

Purpose of Alloying

  • Increase strength and hardness.
  • Improve corrosion resistance.
  • Improve appearance and durability.
  • Change melting point or electrical properties.

Glasses

Glass TypeUse
Sodium silicate glassGeneral glass materials
Borosilicate glassLaboratory glassware; heat resistant
Safety glassAutomobile windshields and windows
Insulating glassThermal and sound insulation

Refractory Materials

Refractories are materials that can withstand very high temperature without melting or deforming.

  • Fire clay: Used in furnaces and kilns.
  • Magnesite: Used in steel industry furnaces.

9. Polymers & Rubber

CO3M3.02

A polymer is a large molecule formed by joining many small molecules called monomers.

Basic Terms

  • Monomer: Small repeating unit.
  • Polymerization: Process of forming polymer from monomers.
  • Homopolymer: Made from one type of monomer.
  • Copolymer: Made from two or more types of monomers.

Types of Polymerization

  • Addition polymerization: Monomers add without by-product. Example: polythene.
  • Condensation polymerization: Monomers join with loss of small molecules. Example: Nylon-66.

Thermoplastics and Thermosetting Plastics

  • Thermoplastics: Soften on heating and harden on cooling. Example: PVC.
  • Thermosetting plastics: Set permanently after heating. Example: Bakelite.

Rubber

Natural rubber is soft and sticky. Vulcanization is heating rubber with sulphur to improve strength, elasticity and durability.

  • Buna-S: Synthetic rubber made from butadiene and styrene.
  • Buna-N: Synthetic rubber made from butadiene and acrylonitrile.

12. Faraday's Laws of Electrolysis

CO4M4.02

Faraday's laws explain the relationship between electricity passed through an electrolyte and the amount of substance deposited or liberated at electrodes.

First Law

The mass of substance deposited at an electrode is directly proportional to the quantity of electricity passed through the electrolyte.

m ∝ Q
Q = I × t
m = ZIt

Where m = mass deposited, Z = electrochemical equivalent, I = current, t = time.

Second Law

When the same quantity of electricity passes through different electrolytes, the masses deposited are proportional to their equivalent weights.

m₁ / m₂ = E₁ / E₂
Conversion: If time is given in minutes, convert it into seconds before using Q = It.

Quick Practice

If 2 A current passes for 100 s, then Q = I × t = 2 × 100 = 200 coulombs.

13. Electroplating & Electrolytic Refining

CO4M4.03

Electrolysis has many industrial applications. Two important applications are electroplating and electrolytic refining.

Electroplating

Electroplating is the process of coating one metal over another metal using electrolysis.

Example: Nickel plating on mild steel.

  • Object to be plated is made cathode.
  • Plating metal is made anode.
  • Suitable salt solution is used as electrolyte.

Uses of Electroplating

  • Protects from corrosion.
  • Improves appearance.
  • Improves hardness and wear resistance.

Electrolytic Refining of Copper

  • Impure copper is used as anode.
  • Pure copper sheet is used as cathode.
  • Copper sulphate solution is used as electrolyte.
  • Pure copper deposits on cathode; impurities collect as anode mud.
Copper Refining
Impure Cu Anode→ Cu²⁺ ions → Pure Cu Cathode+ Anode mud

14. Daniel Cell

CO4M4.04

A Daniel cell is an electrochemical cell that converts chemical energy into electrical energy using a redox reaction.

Construction

  • Zinc rod in zinc sulphate solution.
  • Copper rod in copper sulphate solution.
  • The two solutions are connected by a salt bridge.
  • External wire connects zinc and copper electrodes.

Reactions

At anode: Zn → Zn²⁺ + 2e⁻
At cathode: Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu
Overall: Zn + Cu²⁺ → Zn²⁺ + Cu
Daniel Cell Diagram

Cell Representation

Zn | Zn²⁺ || Cu²⁺ | Cu

15. Corrosion & Prevention

CO4M4.05

Corrosion is the slow destruction of metals by chemical or electrochemical reaction with the environment.

Examples

  • Rusting of iron.
  • Tarnishing of silver.
  • Green coating on copper.

Factors Affecting Corrosion

  • Presence of moisture and oxygen.
  • Presence of salts or acids.
  • Impurities in metal.
  • High temperature.

Corrosion Prevention Methods

MethodMeaningExample
Barrier protectionCovering the metal surfacePainting, oiling
Metal coatingCoating with another metalGalvanizing, tinning
AnodisingProtective oxide layer on aluminiumAluminium parts
Anti-rust solutionChemical coating to reduce rustIron tools
Cathodic protectionMaking the metal act as cathodeUnderground pipelines
Sacrificial Anode Method
Important metal protected+ More active metal attached→ Active metal corrodes first→ Main metal safe

Quick Revision

  • Rusting needs moisture and oxygen.
  • Painting is barrier protection.
  • Sacrificial anode method is a cathodic protection method.
